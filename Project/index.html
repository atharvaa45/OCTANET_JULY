Mission Universe

The past hundred years of astronomy have been characterized by an ever-expanding view of the observable universe. We see many different kinds of stars; billions of stars, from brown dwarfs to super-giants. We also see exploding stars and supernovas, neutron stars, and black holes. Some stars in our own Milky Way galaxy are up to 100,000 light-years away. The Universe is believed to have originated in a powerful event known as the Big Bang, approximately 13.8 billion years ago. The universe is constantly expanding, with galaxies moving away from each other as space itself expands. So, Get ready for liftoff! Learning more about our Universe.

Matter

Matter refers to the physical substance that makes up the observable universe. It includes all the particles that have mass and occupy space, such as atoms, molecules, and larger structures like planets, stars, galaxies, and galaxy clusters.

Energy

Energy is another fundamental component of the universe. It exists in various forms, including kinetic energy (associated with motion), potential energy (stored energy), thermal energy (associated with heat), electromagnetic energy (light and other forms of radiation), and more. Energy is closely related to matter through the famous equation E=mc², which states that mass and energy are interchangeable.

Space and Time

Space is the three-dimensional extent in which matter and energy exist. It provides the framework for everything in the universe. Time is the dimension in which events occur and can be measured. It is closely linked to space, forming a four-dimensional continuum called spacetime. Time allows for the ordering of events and the progression from the past, through the present, to the future.

MilkyWay Galaxy
Our Home

The Milky Way is the galaxy that includes the Solar System, with the name describing the galaxy's appearance from Earth: a hazy band of light seen in the night sky formed from stars that cannot be individually distinguished by the naked eye. The term Milky Way is a translation of the Latin via lactea, from the Greek γαλακτικὸς κύκλος (galaktikòs kýklos), meaning "milky circle".

Black Hole
Super-Massive & Dangerous

A black hole is a region in space where gravity is so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape its gravitational pull. It is formed when a massive star collapses under its own gravity, causing its core to become extremely dense. The collapse occurs when the star's nuclear fuel is exhausted and no longer able to counteract the force of gravity. Black holes come in different sizes. Stellar black holes are formed from the remnants of massive stars and can have a mass several times that of our Sun. Supermassive black holes, on the other hand, are millions or even billions of times more massive than the Sun and are believed to exist at the centers of most galaxies, including our own Milky Way.

The Bing Bang

Dawn
of The Universe

The Big Bang is a scientific theory that describes the origin and evolution of the universe. According to this theory, the universe began as an extremely hot and dense state, and it has been expanding and cooling over billions of years. According to the Big Bang theory, about 13.8 billion years ago, all matter, energy, space, and time in the universe were compressed into an incredibly small, hot, and dense singularity. At this point, the universe started to rapidly expand and cool down. As the universe expanded, matter and energy began to separate and form the building blocks of galaxies, stars, and other structures we observe today.
